Frequently Asked Questions

1. Who can donate blood?
- Everyone from 18 to 60 years old actually volunteers to donate their blood to cure the sick.
- Weigh at least 45kg for women and men. The amount of blood donated each time must not exceed 9ml/kg of body weight and must not exceed 500ml each time.
- Not infected or not having acts of transmitting HIV and other blood-borne infectious diseases.
- The time between two blood donations is 12 weeks for both Men and Women.
- Have identification documents.
2. Who should not donate blood?
- People who are infected or have committed acts at risk of infection with HIV, hepatitis B, hepatitis C, and viruses transmitted through blood transfusion.
- People with chronic diseases: cardiovascular, blood pressure, respiratory, stomach...
3. What tests will my blood be tested on?
- All blood units collected will be tested for blood type (ABO system, Rh system), HIV, hepatitis B virus, hepatitis C virus, syphilis, malaria.
- You will be notified of the results, kept confidential and consulted (free) when the above infections are discovered.
4. What is the current need for blood for treatment in our country?
- Every year our country needs about 1,800,000 units of blood for treatment.
- Blood is needed for daily treatment, emergency care, and prevention of disasters and accidents that require blood transfusions in large quantities.
- Currently, we have met about 54% of blood needs for treatment.
5. Is donating blood harmful to your health?
Donating blood according to your doctor's instructions is not harmful to your health. That has been proven by scientific and practical basis:
* Scientific basis:
- Blood has many components, each component only has a certain life and is always renewed every day. For example: Red blood cells live for 120 days, plasma is regularly replaced and renewed. Scientific evidence shows that if each donation is less than 1/10 of the amount of blood in the body, it is not harmful to health.
- Many research projects have proven that, after donating blood, blood indicators change slightly but are still within normal physiological limits and do not affect the body's daily activities.
* Actual basis:
- In fact, there have been millions of people who have donated blood many times and are still in completely good health. There are people in the world who donate blood more than 400 times. In Vietnam, the person who donated blood the most times donated nearly 100 times and was in completely good health.
- Thus, if each person is in good health, does not have infectious diseases through blood transfusion, and meets blood donation standards, they can donate blood 3-4 times a year, without adversely affecting their health. of yourself, while ensuring good quality and safe blood for patients.
6. Benefits for voluntary blood donors?
Benefits and regimes for voluntary blood donors according to Circular No. 05/2017/TT-BYT Regulating the maximum price and costs for determining the price of a unit of whole blood and blood products meeting standards standard:
- Receive free health examination and consultation.
- Be checked and notified of blood test results (completely confidential): blood type, HIV, hepatitis B virus, hepatitis C virus, syphilis, malaria. In case the blood donor is infected or suspected of these pathogens, a doctor will invite him for health advice.
- Be trained and cared for according to current regulations:
+ On-site snack service: equivalent to 30,000 VND.
+ Travel expense support (in cash): 50,000 VND.
+ Choose to receive gifts in kind with the following values:
A unit of blood with a volume of 250 ml: 100,000 VND.
A unit of blood with a volume of 350 ml: 150,000 VND.
A unit of blood with a volume of 450 ml: 180,000 VND.
+ Obtained a certificate of voluntary blood donation from the Provincial and City Humanitarian Blood Donation Steering Committee. In addition to its honorary value, the blood donation certificate has a blood reimbursement value, the maximum amount of blood reimbursed is equal to the amount of blood donated by the blood donor. This Certificate is valid at hospitals and public medical facilities nationwide.
7. Can you get infected when donating blood?
- The blood collection needle is sterile and can only be used once per person, so it cannot transmit disease to the blood donor.
8. I will donate blood tomorrow, how should I prepare?
- Tonight you should not stay up too late (sleep before 11:00 p.m.).
- You should eat and do not drink alcohol or beer before donating blood.
- Bring ID card, enough identification documents and blood donation card (if any) when donating blood.
9. In what cases should blood donation be delayed?
- People who must delay donating blood for 12 months from the time:
+ Complete recovery after surgical interventions.
+ Recovery from one of the following diseases: malaria, syphilis, tuberculosis, tetanus, encephalitis, meningitis.
+ Complete rabies vaccination after animal bites or injections, blood transfusions, blood products and biological products derived from blood.
+ Giving birth or terminating pregnancy.
- People who have to delay donating blood for 6 months from the time:
+ Tattoos on the skin.
+ Press the earlobe, nose, navel or other body locations.
+ Exposure to blood and body fluids from people at risk of or infected with blood-borne diseases.
+ Recovery from one of the following diseases: typhoid, sepsis, snakebite, thrombophlebitis, thrombophlebitis, osteomyelitis, pancreatitis.
- People who have to delay donating blood for 4 weeks from the time:
+ Recovery from one of the following diseases: gastroenteritis, urinary tract infection, infectious dermatitis, bronchitis, pneumonia, measles, whooping cough, mumps, dengue fever, dysentery, rubella, cholera , mumps.
+ Finish vaccination against rubella, measles, typhoid, cholera, mumps, chickenpox, and BCG.
- People who have to delay donating blood for 7 days from:
+ Recover from one of the following diseases: flu, cold, nasopharyngeal allergy, sore throat, migraine.
+ Vaccinate all types of vaccines, except those specified in Point c Clause 1 and Point b Clause 3 of this Article.
- Some regulations related to occupations and specific activities of blood donors: people who do certain jobs and perform the following specific activities can only donate blood on days off or only perform other tasks: These tasks and activities are at least 12 hours after donating blood:
+ People working at height or at depth: pilots, crane drivers, workers working at height, climbers, miners, sailors, divers.
+ Operators of public transportation: bus drivers, train drivers, ship drivers.
+ Other cases: professional athletes, heavy athletes, heavy exercisers.
10. Feeling unwell after donating blood or showing signs of swelling at the injection site?
After donating blood:
- If you have symptoms of dizziness, fatigue, nausea, etc., immediately contact the blood receiving unit for assistance.
- If you have signs of swelling or edema at the injection site. Please don't worry too much, immediately apply a cold compress to the swollen area and monitor the above signs. If it does not reduce after 24 hours, contact the blood receiving unit for support.
